## Here's the thing.

There is a version of this conversation that happens in almost every engagement. A CISO shows me a slide deck. The deck has a logo on it. A well known platform. A well known name. The slide says: zero trust. The implication is clear. We have arrived.

We have not arrived.

Zero trust is not a thing you buy. It is a way of thinking about access, identity, and trust relationships in a system that is constantly changing. The product is a tool. The posture is the point.


## Why we care.

The term zero trust was coined by John Kindervag at Forrester in 2010. The core principle is simple: never trust, always verify. Do not assume that anything inside your network perimeter is safe. Verify every request, every time, regardless of where it originates.

That principle is sound. What happened next is what always happens: the market turned a principle into a product category. Vendors built platforms and called them zero trust. Buyers purchased them and checked the box. The principle got lost in the procurement.

The result is a generation of organizations that own zero trust products and have zero trust postures that are anything but.


## What we could do.

Start with the principle, not the product. Before any platform evaluation, answer these questions:

- What are you actually trying to protect, and from whom?
- Where are your trust boundaries today, explicit and implicit?
- What does lateral movement look like in your environment?
- How do you currently verify identity at every layer?

The answers shape the architecture. The architecture shapes the tooling. In that order, not the reverse.

When Production Perimeter was built, the assumption was never that the tool would solve the problem. The tool enforced decisions that had already been made about trust, identity, and boundaries. The hard work was upstream: understanding the architecture, mapping the flows, deciding what should and should not be trusted. The software came last.

> The posture precedes the product. Always.


## What the outcome was.

Organizations that approach zero trust as a posture first and a product second end up in a fundamentally different place. They can evaluate tools on their own terms. They can identify gaps that no product fills. They can explain to their board not just what they bought, but how they think.

That is the difference between security theater and security practice. One looks right on a slide. The other holds up when something goes wrong.


## What I learned.

We are still having this conversation. That is the most honest thing I can tell you.

The tooling changes. The frameworks come and go. Zero trust replaced perimeter defense. Something else will replace zero trust. And in every cycle, the same pattern: procurement before principle, product before posture.

What does not change is discipline. Culture. The way a team actually thinks about access, identity, and trust when nobody is watching the slide deck. Those things move slowly. And if they do not keep pace with the tooling, all we are doing is chasing fashions.

New tools come and go. New frameworks, less so. But discipline and culture: those are the ones that hold when something goes wrong.

Design for failure. Execute for success.
